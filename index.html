Impact

Langston Hughes was an influential poem and novel writer, describing the flaws of the civil rights laws. He wrote about the civil injustice and political radicalism. Later in his career, he also wrote about the racism and injustice that African-Americans faced in the era. With the influence that he has through his works, Hughes is able to write about the African-American culture he experiences and show that culture to people around the country. Hughes also perfected the idea of jazz poetry, poetry that had a rhythm and repetition just like in actual jazz.

Timeline

A timeline of Hughes' life

  • February 1, 1902

    Birth of Langston Hughes

    James Langston Hughes was born in Joplin, Missouri to James Hughes and Carrie Langston. His parents soon divorced, with only his mother and grandmother to care for him.

  • 1920 - 1921

    The Start of Hughes' Career

    Once Hughes graduated high school, he was interested in the topic in poetry as his teacher introduced him to popular poets at the time. By 1921, he published his first poem to The Crisis called "The Negro Speaks of the River"

  • 1926–1929

    Full Tuition to Lincoln University

    After Hughes' poem, "The Weary Blues", won a competition, Hughes was granted a full scholarship to study in Lincoln University. Even though Hughes' father resented this, he eventually saw that Hughes has a passion for literature and poetry.

  • 1930

    Changing Topics

    Hughes began writing about the injustice and racism dealt towards African-Americans.

  • 1951

    "Harlem"

    Hughes published one of his most famous poems, “Harlem”, which describes the short-lived American Dream of African Americans.

  • May 22, 1967

    Death

    Hughes passes away due to prostate complications. Today, he is known as an esteemed poet and writer from the Civil Rights Era.
